Do the Adjusting-Amount Procedure and MCQ Measure the Same Construct?

Python reproducibility companion to Wan, Myerson, and Green (2023)

Wan, H., Myerson, J., & Green, L. (2023). Individual differences in degree of discounting: Do different procedures and measures assess the same construct? Behavioural Processes, 208, 104864. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.beproc.2023.104864

Executive summary

This report examines whether the Adjusting-Amount procedure (Adj-Amt) and the Monetary Choice Questionnaire (MCQ) capture the same individual-difference construct.

The published study analyzed 393 adults recruited through Prolific (n = 150) and Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk; n = 243 after attention-check exclusions). Its central findings were:

  1. Both procedures reproduced the magnitude effect. Larger delayed rewards were discounted less steeply in both samples and with both procedures (all published ps < .001).
  2. Individual differences were reliable and convergent. Published between-procedure correlations ranged from .741 to .821.
  3. Convergence did not imply interchangeability. The MCQ produced steeper discounting than Adj-Amt at $30 (p = .003), but the procedures did not differ at $80 (p = .95).

Scientific conclusion. Adj-Amt and MCQ scores capture substantial common variance and are consistent with a shared underlying construct. Their log k estimates should not be treated as numerically interchangeable without considering reward amount, procedure, and recruitment source.

Reproducibility boundary

The archived project code indicates that the publication’s Adj-Amt log k estimates were obtained with a Stan model that treated preference reversals probabilistically; the article describes these estimates as simple-hyperbola fits. This notebook estimates Adj-Amt log k by nonlinear least squares from the analysis-ready subjective values. It reproduces the analysis logic and substantive pattern, but not every published theoretical point estimate. Published statistics are presented explicitly as benchmarks and are never relabeled as newly computed results.

Data availability

Research questions

  1. Do both procedures reproduce the magnitude effect?
  2. Are individual differences reliable across reward amounts and scoring approaches?
  3. Do Adj-Amt and MCQ measure the same construct?
  4. Do the procedures and online samples yield the same absolute degree of discounting?

Participants completed both procedures in counterbalanced order. Adj-Amt measured subjective values for delayed rewards of $30, $80, and $500. The MCQ contained three nine-item sets with delayed amounts of $25-$35, $50-$60, and $75-$85.

Procedure Atheoretical measure Theoretically based measure
Adj-Amt Area under the empirical discounting curve (AuC) log k from a simple hyperbola
MCQ Proportion of larger-delayed choices log k from Kirby’s consistency-scoring method

Lower AuC and delayed-choice proportions indicate steeper discounting. Higher log k indicates steeper discounting, so correlations between atheoretical measures and log k are expected to be negative.

# 2. Score participant-level discounting measures

def calculate_auc(group):
    # Trapezoidal AuC using the four delays common to all Adj-Amt amounts.
    ordered = group.sort_values("iv")
    return np.trapezoid(ordered["value"], ordered["iv"] / 180)


def fit_adj_logk(group):
    # Companion log(k) estimate from a simple hyperbola.
    def log_hyperbola(delay, log_k):
        return -np.log1p(np.exp(log_k) * delay)

    try:
        estimate, _ = curve_fit(
            log_hyperbola,
            group["iv"].to_numpy(),
            np.log(group["value"].to_numpy()),
            p0=[-4],
            maxfev=10_000,
        )
        return float(estimate[0])
    except (RuntimeError, ValueError):
        return np.nan


def score_mcq_logk(group):
    # Kirby consistency score for one participant and MCQ amount set.
    question_k = group["iv"].to_numpy()
    delayed_choice = group["value"].to_numpy()
    consistency = np.array([
        np.sum(
            ((delayed_choice == 0) & (question_k <= candidate_k))
            | ((delayed_choice == 1) & (question_k >= candidate_k))
        )
        for candidate_k in question_k
    ])
    best_k = question_k[consistency == consistency.max()]
    return float(np.log(gmean(best_k)))


adj_rows = []
for (participant_id, provider, amount), group in (
    raw_df[(raw_df["procedure"] == "aa") & (raw_df["iv"] != 730)]

):
    adj_rows.append({
        "id": participant_id,
        "provider": provider,
        "amt": amount,

adj_scores = pd.DataFrame(adj_rows)

3. Benchmark validity: the magnitude effect

Larger delayed rewards are typically discounted less steeply than smaller delayed rewards. Reproducing this magnitude effect establishes that both procedures generated the expected behavioral pattern.

# Group-level nonlinear fits

def r_squared(observed, predicted):
    residual = np.sum((observed - predicted) ** 2)
    total = np.sum((observed - np.mean(observed)) ** 2)
    return 1 - residual / total


def hyperboloid(delay, log_k, scale):
    return 1 / (1 + np.exp(log_k) * delay) ** scale


def logistic_growth(log_question_k, intercept, rate):
    return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(log_question_k - intercept) * rate))


group_summary = (
    raw_df.groupby(["provider", "procedure", "amt", "iv"], as_index=False)
    .agg(mean_value=("value", "mean"), median_value=("value", "median"))
)

adj_fit_rows = []
for (provider, amount), group in group_summary[
    group_summary["procedure"] == "aa"
].groupby(["provider", "amt"]):
    parameters, _ = curve_fit(
        hyperboloid, group["iv"], group["median_value"],
        p0=[-4, 1], maxfev=10_000,
    )
    predicted = hyperboloid(group["iv"].to_numpy(), *parameters)
    adj_fit_rows.append({
        "provider": provider, "amount": amount,
        "r_squared": r_squared(group["median_value"].to_numpy(), predicted),
    })

mcq_fit_rows = []
for (provider, amount), group in group_summary[
    group_summary["procedure"] == "mcq"
].groupby(["provider", "amt"]):
    log_question_k = np.log(group["iv"].to_numpy())
    parameters, _ = curve_fit(
        logistic_growth, log_question_k, group["mean_value"],
        p0=[-4, 1], maxfev=10_000,
    )
    predicted = logistic_growth(log_question_k, *parameters)
    mcq_fit_rows.append({
        "provider": provider, "amount": amount,
        "r_squared": r_squared(group["mean_value"].to_numpy(), predicted),
    })

print("Companion Adj-Amt group fits")
display(pd.DataFrame(adj_fit_rows).round(3))
print("Companion MCQ group fits")
display(pd.DataFrame(mcq_fit_rows).round(3))


# Outcome-appropriate magnitude-effect models with participant-clustered SEs.
amount_tests = []
for provider, data in adj_scores.groupby("provider"):
    model = BetaModel.from_formula("auc ~ C(amt)", data=data).fit(
        disp=False,
        cov_type="cluster",
        cov_kwds={"groups": data["id"]},
    )
    amount_tests.append({
        "provider": provider,
        "procedure": "Adj-Amt",
        "contrast": "largest versus smallest amount",
        "p_value": model.pvalues["C(amt)[T.3]"],
    })

for provider, data in mcq_scores.groupby("provider"):
    model = glm(
        "delayed_choice_prop ~ C(amt)",
        data=data,
        family=sm.families.Binomial(),
        freq_weights=np.repeat(9, len(data)),
    ).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": data["id"]})
    amount_tests.append({
        "provider": provider,
        "procedure": "MCQ",
        "contrast": "largest versus smallest amount",
        "p_value": model.pvalues["C(amt)[T.3]"],
    })

print("Companion magnitude-effect tests")
display(pd.DataFrame(amount_tests))

The article reported a significant magnitude effect in every procedure-by-sample analysis (all ps < .001).

4. Reliability and convergent validity

Scores at different reward amounts were treated as alternative forms. Positive correlations across amounts therefore indicate that each procedure preserves individual differences as reward magnitude changes.

Only two conditions were comparable between procedures: Adj-Amt $30 with MCQ $25-$35, and Adj-Amt $80 with MCQ $75-$85.

# Within-procedure reliability

def amount_correlations(data, measure):
    wide = data.pivot(index=["id", "provider"], columns="amt", values=measure)
    rows = []
    for provider, group in wide.groupby(level="provider"):
        corr = group.droplevel("provider").corr()
        rows.append({
            "provider": provider,
            "small-medium": corr.loc[1, 2],
            "small-large": corr.loc[1, 3],
            "medium-large": corr.loc[2, 3],
        })
    return pd.DataFrame(rows)


reliability = []
for procedure, data, measure, label in [
    ("Adj-Amt", adj_scores, "auc", "AuC"),
    ("Adj-Amt", adj_scores, "log_k_companion", "Companion log k"),
    ("MCQ", mcq_scores, "delayed_choice_prop", "Delayed-choice proportion"),
    ("MCQ", mcq_scores, "log_k_companion", "Companion log k"),
]:
    table = amount_correlations(data, measure)
    table.insert(0, "measure", label)
    table.insert(0, "procedure", procedure)
    reliability.append(table)

print("Companion alternate-forms correlations")
display(pd.concat(reliability, ignore_index=True).round(3))

The published within-procedure correlations were uniformly significant and ranged from .737 to .925. The atheoretical between-procedure correlations reproduce the published values because they are calculated directly from the same observed responses. Small differences in theoretical correlations are expected because the companion Adj-Amt estimates use nonlinear least squares rather than the publication’s Stan model.

5. Absolute differences: convergence is not equivalence

The publication compared log k values using a 2 (sample) x 2 (amount) x 2 (procedure) factorial ANOVA.

Published effect F(1, 1564) p
Amount 38.48 < .001
Procedure 4.42 .04
Sample 130.80 < .001
Procedure x Amount 8.20 .004

No other interactions were significant (all ps > .26). The interaction showed that the MCQ produced steeper discounting than Adj-Amt at $30 (p = .003), whereas the procedures did not differ at $80 (p = .95).

Wording discrepancy in the published article. Section 3.4 reports that Adj-Amt log k was more negative at $30 and states that this reflects steeper discounting on the MCQ. A later sentence in the Discussion states the opposite direction. Because larger log k values indicate steeper discounting, the numerical result supports the Section 3.4 interpretation. This report follows the numerical result and flags the discrepancy rather than silently resolving it.

The companion model below uses the transparent nonlinear-least-squares estimates. It is a sensitivity analysis, not a replacement for the published Stan-based ANOVA.

# Companion factorial model and within-amount paired comparisons

adj_anova = adj_scores[adj_scores["amt"].isin([1, 2])].copy()
adj_anova["amount"] = np.where(adj_anova["amt"] == 1, "$30", "$80")
adj_anova["procedure"] = "Adj-Amt"
adj_anova = adj_anova.rename(columns={"log_k_companion": "log_k"})

mcq_anova = mcq_scores[mcq_scores["amt"].isin([1, 3])].copy()
mcq_anova["amount"] = np.where(mcq_anova["amt"] == 1, "$30", "$80")
mcq_anova["procedure"] = "MCQ"
mcq_anova = mcq_anova.rename(columns={"log_k_companion": "log_k"})

anova_data = pd.concat([
    adj_anova[["id", "provider", "amount", "procedure", "log_k"]],
    mcq_anova[["id", "provider", "amount", "procedure", "log_k"]],
], ignore_index=True)

companion_model = ols(
    "log_k ~ C(provider) * C(amount) * C(procedure)", data=anova_data
).fit()

print("Companion factorial ANOVA")
display(sm.stats.anova_lm(companion_model, typ=2).round(3))

paired_results = []
for amount, group in common_scores.groupby("amount"):
    test = ttest_rel(group["adj_log_k"], group["mcq_log_k"])
    paired_results.append({
        "amount": amount,
        "mean_adj_log_k": group["adj_log_k"].mean(),
        "mean_mcq_log_k": group["mcq_log_k"].mean(),
        "paired_t": test.statistic,
        "p_value": test.pvalue,
    })

print("Companion paired procedure comparisons")
display(pd.DataFrame(paired_results).round(3))

6. Recruitment source and data quality

No Prolific participant failed an attention check, whereas 32 of 275 MTurk recruits did. Even after those exclusions, the MTurk sample contained substantially more extremely steep discounters.

# Extreme-discounting patterns reported in the article

adj_participant = (
    adj_scores.groupby(["id", "provider"], as_index=False)
    .agg(mean_auc=("auc", "mean"))

mcq_participant = (
    mcq_scores.groupby(["id", "provider"], as_index=False)
    .agg(total_delayed_choices=("delayed_choices", "sum"))

distribution_rows = []
for provider, group in participant_distributions.groupby("provider"):

        "MCQ delayed choices <= 3, %": 100 * (group["total_delayed_choices"] <= 3).mean(),

The platform differences cannot be attributed causally to recruitment source because the samples also differed in age, education, and ethnicity. They nevertheless show why attention checks and distributional diagnostics are part of measurement quality rather than merely administrative screening.

7. Interpretation and research guidance

Research priority Stronger practical fit Reason
Brief online assessment MCQ Twenty-seven fixed items and established scoring
Rewards above $85 Adj-Amt Flexible delayed amounts
Individual discounting functions Adj-Amt Direct subjective-value estimates across delays
Probability, loss, or nonmonetary extensions Adj-Amt or a separately validated questionnaire The standard MCQ is specific to small delayed monetary gains
Comparing absolute log k values across studies Neither without calibration Procedure and amount can shift the level of log k

Conclusions

  • Adj-Amt and MCQ show strong convergent validity across two online samples.
  • The conclusion is robust to theoretical and atheoretical scoring approaches.
  • The procedures are not automatically interchangeable at the level of absolute log k.
  • Recruitment source and attention-check performance are part of the measurement context.
  • Procedure choice should follow the scientific question, outcome domain, reward range, and required measurement detail.

Scope and limitations

The evidence concerns delayed monetary gains in two United States online samples. It does not establish equivalence for delayed losses, probabilistic outcomes, combined delay-and-probability outcomes, nonmonetary rewards, or other populations. Correlations establish shared variance, not causal validity or score identity.
